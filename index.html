What is AI in terms of the course we will go through this semester? AI includes diverse topics such as problem-solving, reasoning, theorem proving, learning, planning, knowledge, representation, perception and robotics, agents, and, more recently, deep learning. Also, AI ethics has become a big topic lately.

 

 

There are two broad approaches, or stances, in AI. The first is strong AI. In strong AI, people want to build a machine capable of thought, consciousness, and emotions equaling that of humans. They assume that humans are no more than elaborate computers. Because of this, they view the computer model as the mind.

 

 

Whereas on the opposite end, there is weak AI. In weak AI, people want to develop a series of human and animal intelligence and then test these series by building working models, usually in the form of computer programs or robots. Here, the working models are viewed as tools to aid in our understanding of intelligence, and because of this, they do not claim that the machines themselves are capable of thought, consciousness, and emotions.

 

 

There are problems with both approaches. For strong AI, it is hard to determine if something is truly consciously intelligent or not, so this is called the other minds problem in philosophy. I know I am conscious, but I can never tell whether anyone else, including us, or our families, is conscious.

 

 

For weak AI, the problem is that the stated goal limits the utility of the results. Also, it is hard to achieve general usefulness as in true intelligence.

 

 

So we can ask how we should do AI. Think about a different domain, aerospace engineering, in which we have succeeded. So why should not we just engineer AI in the same way that people engineered airplanes? So first consider flight. In this case, the goal is very simple, and we know when a thing is flying. For example, if it flows in the air and moves a certain number of miles, then we can see that it is flying.

However, intelligence is very different. The goal is very complex and hard to define clearly, as we have seen earlier. Also, intelligence is a collection of many different abilities. There are many ways to meet a single clear goal in the case of flight, but there can be only a small number of ways to simultaneously meet a huge number of unclearly defined goals in the case of intelligence.

 

 

So why should we not follow the plane model? So even for the aerospace engineering approach, certain things may seem physically impossible given the aerospace engineering theory. But we know they do fly, and we cannot use theories or engineering products based on jet turbines to explain how flies achieve this impossible feat. An interesting recent discovery was that flies gyrate their wings to generate a vortex to create greater lift.

 

 

So the lesson here is that if we fail to build the impossible, study an existing solution. Here is an illustration from the paper which describes this finding.
